The Product

The sportsbook's sibling.
Sport, traded as contracts.

Every market is a binary contract on a sporting outcome — buy YES or NO between 1¢ and 99¢, where the price is the probability, and every contract settles at $1 or $0. Sports has driven roughly 80% of volume on the leading exchange since 2024, analysts project $5–10 billion in trading on this World Cup alone, and the operators have noticed: DraftKings bought the Railbird exchange, FanDuel partnered with CME, and the NHL, MLB and MLS have licensed their trademarks into these markets. This is a betting product — and it takes a betting operator to run it well.

Odds

No overround. Your fee.

Exchange pricing inverts the book: the crowd makes the price and the venue earns a fee, not an overround. On the leading exchange that fee is 7% × p × (1−p) per contract — peaking at just 1.75¢ on a 50¢ market, with market makers paying roughly 75% less. A structure built to reward whoever quotes the tightest price. Winning there isn't about baking in margin — it's knowing the true probability better than the crowd, which is what an odds-compiling desk has always done.

Trading

Liquidity is the product.

Every trade on an exchange is a maker matched with a taker — and the maker side is where the operator lives. Resting orders create the liquidity; fee schedules reward them with roughly 75% lower rates. Seeding books on every fixture, quoting both sides, holding spreads to a cent or two, and deciding what flow to keep versus lay off is a trading desk's discipline. White-label providers sell “no trading desk required” — fine for a media brand. For an operator, the desk is the edge, and outsourcing it means outsourcing your margin.

Risk Management

Exchange risk is
different risk.

There's no book to balance when the crowd holds both sides — the risk moves to positions, integrity and settlement. The majors treat it that way: the leading exchange runs Sportradar's UFDS AI monitoring and shares threats through the Sportradar Integrity Exchange, the same surveillance stack the big sportsbooks sit on. The discipline that protects a book protects an exchange too — it just watches different numbers.

Live / In-Play

Contracts that trade with the game.

In-play is where event contracts feel most like betting: the price is the live win probability, repricing with every goal, break and momentum swing. The leading exchange now runs its in-play markets on official league data and live odds through Sportradar — because at game speed, settlement can't wait for a second source. Players trade out mid-game the way they'd cash out a bet, and the matching engine has to price, match and grade at peak load without seizing up.
